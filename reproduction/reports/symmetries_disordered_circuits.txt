 1     Abstract



 2         A major challenge in neuroscience is reconciling idealized theoretical models with complex, heteroge-
 3     neous experimental data. We address this challenge through continuous-attractor networks, which model
 4     how neural circuits represent continuous variables such as head direction or spatial location through
 5     collective dynamics. Classical continuous-attractor models rely on continuous symmetry in the recurrent
 6     weights to generate a manifold of stable states, predicting tuning curves that are identical up to shifts.
 7     However, mouse head-direction cells exhibit substantial heterogeneity in their responses, seemingly in-
 8     compatible with this classical picture. We demonstrate that mammalian circuits could nevertheless rely on
 9     the same dynamical mechanisms as classical continuous-attractor models. We construct recurrent neural
10     networks directly from experimental head-direction tuning curves that exhibit quasi-continuous-attractor
11     dynamics, then develop a statistical generative process quantitatively capturing the structure of tuning
12     heterogeneity. This enables large-N analysis, where we show through dynamical mean-field theory that
13     these networks become equivalent to classical ring-attractor models, with Mexican-hat interactions and
14     continuous symmetry that is spontaneously broken, leading to bump states. In the seemingly disordered
15     weights, the continuous symmetry essential to classical models is reflected through eigenvalue degenera-
16     cies, positioning spectral structure as a target for detecting continuous-attractor circuits in connectome
17     data. We extend this framework to two-dimensional symmetries, constructing grid-cell models that
18     similarly reduce to classical toroidal attractors. Our work demonstrates that the dynamical mechanisms
19     of classical continuous-attractor models may operate not only in small brains or idealized systems but
20     also in complex mammalian circuits.

107     1     Introduction
108     Organisms from flies to humans maintain mental representations of continuous variables. These include
109     navigationally relevant variables, such as self-orientation and location, as well as more abstract cognitive
110     variables. Continuous-attractor models explain how networks of interacting neurons, each individually
111     lacking memory, can maintain these representations through their collective dynamics. In these models,
112     synaptic weights are configured to generate a manifold of stable states that matches the group structure of
113     the encoded variable, providing persistent working memory of this variable. Furthermore, by incorporating
114     velocity inputs that modify network states, such systems can integrate these inputs.
115         Classical continuous-attractor models generate marginally stable manifolds of fixed points through
116     three ingredients (Fig. 2A, B) [1]. First, neurons must transform input currents into firing rates nonlinearly.
117     Second, the recurrent weights must support Turing pattern formation, typically through a Mexican-hat
118     profile with local excitation and longer-range inhibition. Third, these weights must possess a continuous
119     symmetry corresponding to the encoded variable: one-dimensional circular symmetry for head direction,
120     two-dimensional symmetry for grid-cell responses, and so on. The essential dynamical mechanism is
121     that pattern formation spontaneously breaks the continuous symmetry, and the resulting activity patterns
122     form a continuous manifold with the same symmetry as the weights and thus as the encoded variable.
123     As a consequence of this mechanism, the tuning curves of individual neurons (i.e., their responses as a
124     function of the encoded variable) are identical up to neuron-specific shifts (Fig. 2C).
125         A canonical example of continuous-attractor models is the ring-attractor model for one-dimensional
126     circular variables, such as head direction or visual-stimulus orientation [2–8]. The experimental discovery
127     of a ring-attractor circuit in the brain of Drosophila melanogaster several decades after the formulation
128     of this model provided a notable validation of theoretical approaches. In the central complex, a region
129     called the ellipsoid body has an anatomical ring structure that produces a localized bump of activity
130     that accurately tracks the animal’s orientation, appearing to be a nearly exact instantiation of a classical
131     ring-attractor model [9–11]. Ring-attractor-like circuitry has also been discovered in zebrafish [12].
132         In this paper, we investigate how mammalian brains might implement stable representations of
133     continuous variables. Rodents, for example, possess neurons that encode head direction, analogous to
134     the heading representation in flies, in regions including the anterodorsal nucleus of the thalamus and
135     the postsubiculum [13–15]. Similarly, recordings from the medial entorhinal cortex have revealed grid
136     cells that represent spatial location through hexagonally arranged firing fields, with each cell’s activity
137     peaking at the vertices of a hexagonal lattice that spans the environment [16, 17]. An open question is
138     whether these are examples of the classical continuous-attractor mechanism described above. Evidence
139     suggests that the intrinsic circuit structure constrains neuronal activity to low-dimensional manifolds, and
140     that this manifold confinement persists even during sleep, a hallmark prediction of continuous-attractor
141     models. Such structured activity patterns have been demonstrated in both rodent head-direction cells,
142     where activity is confined to a manifold with ring topology [18, 19], and grid cells, where activity traces
143     out a manifold with toroidal topology [20–22].
144         While mammalian neural circuits possess the requisite nonlinear neurons and strong excitatory and
145     inhibitory recurrent connectivity, their tuning to continuous variables is markedly different. Instead of
146     having tuning curves that are identical up to neuron-specific shifts—a direct consequence of continuous
147     symmetry in the weights of classical models—mammalian circuits show diverse response profiles. This
148     tuning heterogeneity is evident in the rodent head-direction system, which we examine in detail; in the
149     medial entorhinal cortex, where neurons display a continuous spectrum of “grid scores” [23–25], with
150     only a small subset exhibiting the periodicity predicted by classical models; and elsewhere in the brain
151     [26].
152          These observations raise two interconnected questions:

153         1. What is the nature of heterogeneous tuning in biological neural circuits?

154         2. To what extent are classical models, based on dynamical breaking of continuous symmetry, relevant
155            to such circuits?

156     Question (2) has a practical corollary in light of the nascent but growing availability of large-scale
157     electron-microscopy reconstructions of detailed synaptic connectivity, or “connectomes,” in mammalian
158     cortex [27, 28]. In classical continuous-attractor models, the connectivity exhibits easily identifiable
159     structure (e.g., a circulant weight matrix for ring-attractor models), providing clear signatures to search
160     for in connectomics data. In heterogeneous mammalian circuits, it remains unknown which connectivity
161     features, if any, are conserved from classical models and, relatedly, what signatures should be targeted in
162     connectome analyses.
163         Recent work has begun to separately address aspects of these questions. Regarding question (1),
164     Mainali et al. [29] demonstrated that sampling tuning curves from a Gaussian process and passing
165     them through a nonlinear activation function provides a quantitatively accurate model of topological
166     features of hippocampal place-cell firing fields across dimensionalities and environments. Relatedly,
167     Gaussian process-based latent-variable models of neuronal tuning that allow for unsupervised discovery
168     of topologically nontrivial latent variables have been proposed [30, 31].
169          Regarding question (2), various constructions of network models with heterogeneous continuous-
170     attractor manifolds have been proposed, though no explicit correspondence to classical continuous-
171     attractor models has been established. Darshan and Rivkind [32] applied an earlier result of Rivkind and
172     Barak [33] to circular manifolds, showing that a combination of high- and low-rank weights generates
173     ring attractors with heterogeneous tuning. Pollock and Jazayeri [34] developed methods for constructing
174     recurrent neural networks with prescribed dynamics, providing another approach for generating ring
175     attractors with heterogeneous tuning. Within the broader context of low-rank recurrent neural networks,
176     Mastrogiuseppe and Ostojic [35] and Beiran et al. [36] showed that random low-rank weights with
177     appropriate correlations can generate ring attractors with amplitude variability in tuning. While these
178     works provide specific constructions of continuous-attractor models with heterogeneous tuning, such
179     constructibility is guaranteed on general grounds by the universal approximation capability of recurrent
180     neural networks (Sec. A5) [37]. These approaches neither establish any relationship to classical models
181     nor identify which connectivity features are conserved between classical and heterogeneous models.
182     Furthermore, they have not been constrained by the statistical structure of tuning heterogeneity observed
183     in experiential data.
184         This paper jointly addresses questions (1) and (2) within a unified framework, demonstrating that
185     heterogeneous mammalian circuits could rely on the same symmetry-based dynamical mechanism as
186     classical continuous-attractor models. Briefly, we construct recurrent neural networks that generate
187     experimentally observed tuning curves as stable attractor manifolds, develop a statistical generative
188     process capturing biological tuning heterogeneity (question (1)), show through dynamical mean-field
189     theory that these heterogeneous networks are equivalent to classical models at large N (question (2)),
190     and identify spectral degeneracies as the key conserved connectivity signature between classical and
191     heterogeneous models.

210     motivated by statistical analysis of the experimental tuning-curve distribution. The resulting model
211     reproduces the statistical structure of biological head-direction cells with quantitative accuracy across
212     multiple measures, enabling analysis of network properties at scales reflecting mammalian neural circuits.
213         Using the generative process, we analyze the structural properties of optimal weight matrices at
214     large N . Despite appearing disordered, these matrices reflect an underlying circular symmetry that
215     manifests as doubly degenerate eigenvalues. This symmetry, however, is realized differently than in
216     classical ring-attractor models [2] and in other models [32]. While classical models embed circular
217     symmetry through orderly Fourier eigenvectors across neurons, our model achieves the same symmetry
218     through disordered, random vectors. This distinction has direct implications for connectomics: eigenvalue
219     degeneracies, rather than eigenvector structure, may provide a more reliable signature for identifying
220     continuous-attractor circuits in mammalian brains. We formalize the relationships between continuous
221     symmetries, eigenvalue degeneracies, and symmetries of the dynamics in Sec. A1.
222          Finally, we develop a dynamical mean-field theory to analyze these networks at large N . This reveals
223     a central result, answering question (2): the network dynamics become equivalent to a classical ring-
224     attractor model. The mean-field description recovers all essential features of classical continuous-attractor
225     models—a nonlinear activation function, Mexican-hat interactions, and continuous symmetry—with
226     spontaneous symmetry breaking generating stable bump states through a Turing instability. The effective
227     activation function has interesting differences from a conventional activation function that we discuss.
228     Angular-velocity inputs are faithfully integrated within these mean-field dynamics. We extend this
229     framework to higher dimensions by modeling grid cells in the medial entorhinal cortex, which are thought
230     to comprise a toroidal attractor manifold.

240         We analyzed Ndata = 1533 excitatory neurons, pooled across 31 mice, that were identified as head-
241     direction cells by Duszkiewicz et al. [19]. These cells were classified based on encoding significant
242     head-direction information relative to a time-reversed control distribution, without imposing constraints
243     on tuning-curve shapes such as unimodality. We constructed tuning curves by dividing head-direction
244     angles into 100 bins and computing occupancy-normalized spike counts for each neuron. This binning
245     introduces high-frequency artifacts (Fig. 2D, “partition 1” and “partition 2”). To reduce noise while
246     preserving structure, we applied cross-validated smoothing using a Gaussian kernel. For each neuron,
247     we split the recording into two temporal partitions, computed separate tuning curves, and selected the
248     kernel width that maximized the cross-partition Poisson log-likelihood. Optimal smoothing widths were
249     correlated across partitions (r = 0.16, p < 0.001). The final tuning curve was obtained by averaging the
250     two smoothed partition curves (Fig. 2D, “smoothed”). All tuning curves were normalized to unit mean
251     across head direction θ to enable comparison across neurons and animals. These smoothed, normalized
252     tuning curves form the basis for subsequent analyses.

253     2.1 Failure of classical ring-attractor models
254     Classical ring-attractor models (Fig. 2A, B) predict tuning curves that are identical up to neuron-specific
255     shifts (Fig. 2C), a direct consequence of circular symmetry in the weights. However, tuning curves of
256     mouse head-direction cells exhibit significant heterogeneity, with neurons displaying variable numbers of
257     irregularly spaced peaks (Fig. 2D). To quantify this heterogeneity, we aligned tuning curves within each
258     of the 31 mice by their circular centers of mass and computed the mean profile (Fig. 2E) and fluctuations
259     (standard deviation) around this within-mouse mean profile (Fig. 2F). While the mean profiles were
260     unimodal and symmetric, the fluctuations were comparable in magnitude to the means, starkly violating
261     the classical prediction of zero fluctuations. Both the mean and fluctuation profiles exhibited remarkable
262     consistency across mice: the gray curves (individual mice) closely track the black curve (cross-mouse
263     average) in both cases. This consistency suggests a universal statistical structure (Sec. 3).

264     2.2 Network construction
265     The observed heterogeneity rules out the possibility that the recorded neurons themselves comprise a
266     classical ring-attractor circuit. To test whether intrinsic recurrent connectivity alone can generate the

268     the experimental tuning curves using an optimization principle. This setup is summarized in Sec. B3.1
269     (Setting 1).

332     2.3 Dynamics of the data-derived network model
333     We now examine the dynamics of the data-derived network model, focusing on its ability to generate
334     a quasi-continuous ring attractor manifold. We used regularizer λ = 10−6 and uniform inhibition with
335     strength c = 1 to stabilize the mean activity mode (Sec. B4.4). For visualization, we show the c = 0
336     weights in Fig. 3. The effects of c and λ on the dynamics are shown in Fig. B2.
337         As described in Sec. 1, classical ring-attractor models rely on circular symmetry in the weights to
338     generate a continuous manifold of fixed points (Fig. 2A). The optimized weights Jij do not exhibit this
339     symmetry in an obvious way. When neurons are arranged in arbitrary order, the weight matrix Jij appears,
340     necessarily, unstructured (Fig. 3A). However, when neurons are arranged according to their circular
341     centers of mass, an approximate Mexican-hat structure is revealed (Fig. 3B). One can sort neurons in a
342     similar manner using the singular vectors of the weights (Sec. A3.3) [51].
343        In this work, we are interested in quasi-continuous-attractor dynamics defined through a separation-of-
344    timescales criterion. Specifically, we seek an invariant manifold with fast, attractive normal flow and slow
345    tangential flow (this dynamical definition is more robust than counting discrete fixed points, which can be
346    unreliable indicators of attractor quality) [52]. Due to the overparameterized nature of the optimization
347    problem, the selected weights produced vanishingly small values of τ ∂t xi (t) everywhere on the target
348    manifold (Fig. B10). However, achieving near-zero flow on the manifold alone does not guarantee
349    quasi-continuous-attractor dynamics, which requires that network activity both remains confined to this
350    manifold during evolution and returns to it when perturbed away. The uniform inhibition stabilizes the
351    (N − 1)-dimensional hyperplane on which the neuron-averaged firing rate is unity, but does not guarantee
352    confinement to the one-dimensional target manifold embedded within this hyperplane.
353          Despite not being explicitly encouraged within the optimization problem, the optimal weights
354     empirically produce quasi-continuous-attractor dynamics. Initializing the system at regularly spaced
355     points along the target manifold perturbed by noise, we observe rapid convergence back to the man-
356     ifold within ∼1 s (Fig. 3F), followed by persistence over 30 s. This order-of-magnitude separation
357     between convergence and drift timescales (Fig. 3E) exemplifies quasi-continuous attractor behavior.
358     This stability results from the minimum-norm prescription for the weights (i.e., it is implicitly encour-
359     aged). Small weights ensure that most eigenvalues of the Jacobian evaluated on the target manifold,
360     ∂xj (t) [τ ∂t xi (t)] |x(t)=x⋆ (θ) = −δij + Jij ϕ′ (x⋆j (θ)), are substantially negative, corresponding to rapid re-
361     laxation back to the manifold. Non-minimum-norm constructions by definition add structure to the weight
362     subspace unconstrained by tuning curves, potentially hindering stability. For example, the construction of
363     Darshan and Rivkind [32] generates tuning heterogeneity by operating near chaotic instability (Sec. 5.4,

408          We propose a general structure in which tuning curves are generated by passing samples from a

414     3.1 Evidence for distributional circular symmetry
415     Even when individual tuning curves are heterogeneous, they may arise from a generative process with
416     underlying circular symmetry. Such a symmetry would have two implications. First, it would enable a
417     simplification or “inductive bias” in the generative process itself. Second, it would suggest a mechanism
418     through which circular symmetry could emerge at the population level, despite the disorder present in
419     single-neuron responses.
420         To test this possibility, we first examined the distribution of center-of-mass angles of tuning curves
421     within each mouse (Fig. 4A). Relative phase relationships cannot be meaningfully compared between
422     neurons recorded in different animals, necessitating single-mouse analysis. Visual inspection suggests
423     that these distributions could be uniform (Fig. 4B), which we confirmed using the Kuiper test for circular
424     uniformity. Across 31 mice, only 2 sessions showed significant (p < 0.05) deviations from uniformity
425     before multiple-comparison correction (uncorrected p-values ranging from 0.044 to 0.97). After applying
426     the Benjamini-Hochberg correction for multiple comparisons, no sessions showed significant deviations
427     from uniformity (corrected p-values ranging from 0.59 to 0.97). This uniform distribution of center-of-
428     mass angles is consistent with circular symmetry in the distribution of tuning curves.
429         As a stronger test of this distributional symmetry, we examined the two-point correlation function
430     of tuning curves, C ϕ (θ, θ′ ) (Eq. 4). When computed for increasingly large random subsets of neurons
431     (again, within individual mice), this function appears to approach a circulant form (Fig. 4C), consistent
432     with circular symmetry. The same behavior is seen in normalized correlation functions (i.e., correlation
433     rather than covariance functions), the eigenvectors of which converge to Fourier modes (Fig. B4), as
434     expected for circulant matrices.

483     4     Large-network limit: weights
484     Inspired by the success of the “Gaussian plus nonlinearity” generative process in capturing tuning curve
485     statistics, we now use a similar generative process to study optimal weight matrices and network dynamics
486     at large N , typical of mammalian circuits. We retain the input-current correlation function Γx (∆θ) with
487     rotational symmetry and Fourier decay scale σ. For analytical tractability, we adopt the saturating
                                               √
488     activation function ϕ(x) = 1 + erf(β πx/2), where β controls response sharpness. This choice enables
489     closed-form evaluation of certain integrals. From a dynamical perspective, saturation at 0 and 2 for
490     large negative and positive inputs, respectively, precludes runaway activity, removing the need for the
491     uniform inhibition parameter c used in Sec. 2.3. Finally, since we have eliminated the soft threshold b
492     used in Sec. 3.2, firing rates are non-sparse over θ and the normalization functional Z[x⋆ ] is not needed
493     to amplify small tuning features. This error-function generative process has two parameters, σ and β, and
494     is summarized in Sec. B3.1 (Setting 3).
495         We now analyze the optimal weight matrix J at large N (in this section, we use bold symbols for
496     vectors and matrices). We first point out that, by virtue of the distributional circular symmetry in the
497     generative process, the target manifold possesses a circular geometry at large N ; that is, the distributional
498     symmetry becomes a geometric symmetry. We then show that the optimal weights that encode the
499     target manifold reflect this circular geometry in their eigenvalues, which exhibit doublet degeneracy.
500     A general analysis of the relationships between continuous symmetries, eigenvalue degeneracies, and
501     symmetries of the dynamics is provided in Sec. A1. This spectral structure addresses a corollary to
502     question (2), identifying which connectivity features are conserved between classical and heterogeneous
503     continuous-attractor models. Details are provided in Sec. A3.2. Note that while we analyze the optimal
504     weight matrix using the generative process, the same structure derived here holds in the data-derived
505     weight matrix (Fig. B5).

559     4.2 Disordered vs. ordered embeddings and implications for connectome analysis
560     Because the spectrum of J is, at large N , identical to that of a transpose-symmetric circulant matrix
561     (as in a classical ring-attractor model), the difference between such a matrix and J must lie in the
562     eigenvectors. For circulant weights, the eigenvectors are Fourier modes: in our notation, V ϕ = V x = F .
563     The eigenvalue structure (real, doubly degenerate) combined with Fourier eigenvectors produces a

565     V ϕ , obscuring the structure present in the spectrum.
566         We now describe the nature of these disordered eigenvectors more precisely. Recall that, as N → ∞,
567     V x and V ϕ contain Fourier coefficients of tuning curves (scaled to have unit variance). The elements of
568     V x (whose i-th row contains the Fourier coefficients of x⋆i (θ)) exhibit statistical independence across
569     both the rows and columns. This double independence arises because each neuron’s tuning curve is
570     sampled independently from the same generative process (independence across rows, i = 1, . . . , N )
571     and the Gaussian process construction implies that different Fourier coefficients are uncorrelated by
572     definition (independence across columns, n = 0, . . . , 2D). While the elements of V ϕ (whose i-th row
573     contains the Fourier coefficients of ϕ⋆i (θ)) are similarly independent across rows, they are not independent
574     across columns. This is because applying the nonlinear activation function in θ-space induces higher-
575     order correlations in Fourier space, although pairwise correlations vanish due to rotation invariance.
576     Equivalently, ϕ⋆i (θ) constitutes a non-Gaussian process, which generically has non-independent Fourier
577     coefficients.
578         This disorder has important implications for connectome analysis. Consider the challenge of detecting
579     ring-attractor structure in an observed synaptic weight matrix. A common method is spectral embedding,
580     which involves computing the top eigenvectors of symmetric matrices derived from the weights, assigning
581     coordinates to each neuron based on these eigenvectors, and visualizing the resulting coordinates to detect
582     geometric structure. Candidate matrices include J J T and J T J , which measure similarity of neuronal
583     inputs and outputs, respectively. Their eigenvectors are the left and right singular vectors of J , converging
584     to V x and V ϕ as N → ∞, respectively. Although this method successfully reveals rings in circulant
585     matrices, where the eigenvectors are Fourier modes, it fails for our model because both V x and V ϕ are
586     disordered (Fig. 7F, Fig. B6). Despite V ϕ having some structure in the form of higher-order correlations
587     between entries (Fig. B6), the resulting scatter plots appear similarly disordered to those from V x . In
588     Sec. A3.3, we attempt to extract information about the latent ring structure from the eigenvectors despite
589     their disorder, finding that this approach hallucinates spurious patterns from low-rank structure alone.
590     The ring-attractor models of Beiran et al. [36] and Mastrogiuseppe and Ostojic [35] and of Pollock and
591     Jazayeri [34] also feature disordered embeddings; one could also construct such a disordered-embedding
592     ring-attractor model within the Neural Engineering Framework (see Barak and Romani [8], though they
593     used an ordered embedding; Sec. A5).
594         A natural alternative model, also explored in the data-derived network case (Sec. 2.3, Fig. 3D,I)
595               which a circulant weight matrix is added to a background of independent, random weights.

598     eigenvectors arise in more sophisticated models where the added low-rank component is correlated with

602     ring-attractor structures in synaptic weight matrices, such as those from future connectome data. Unlike
603     eigenvectors, this spectral structure is conserved between classical continuous-attractor models and
604     various other models since this degeneracy directly encodes the geometry of the activity manifold.

647     saturation. The global gain control in GQ (h) is necessary to stabilize the system, compensating for the
648     absence of local saturation. Despite these differences, both mechanisms prevent runaway activity through
649     gain control that depends on the instantaneous state of the system and are therefore similar in this regard.
650     As discussed below, this instantaneous state dependence is not a universal feature of dynamical mean-field
651     theories.

698     5.4 A taxonomy of dynamical mean-field theories
699     One way heterogeneous representations could arise in a recurrent neural network is through the combina-
700     tion of random initialization and task-driven learning. In this spirit, Rivkind and Barak [33] analyzed a
701     two-stage reservoir-computing process [56, 57]: first, a low-rank addition to random background weights
702     is learned to produce a desired set of discrete fixed points; then, the system operates autonomously in
703     closed-loop mode. They computed a response function of the closed-loop system at large N whose poles
704     control local stability around fixed points. We extended this analysis by deriving the dynamical mean-field
705     description of the closed-loop dynamics using a path-integral formulation (Supplement: Dynamics of
706     learned attractors). This description captures the global nonlinear dynamics, extending beyond the local
707     linear analysis of Rivkind and Barak [33]. This theory involves order parameters given by overlaps of
708     network states with learned patterns, analogous to order parameters in Hopfield networks. We recover the
709     response-function poles computed by Rivkind and Barak [33] as eigenvalues governing the linearized
710     dynamics of these overlaps.
711         The structure of the dynamical mean-field theory depends on the nature of the feedback weights that
712     embed fixed points into neuronal space. For Gaussian feedback weights, the overlap dynamics reduce to
713     an effective recurrent neural network with interactions determined by pattern statistics. For non-Gaussian
714     feedback weights, such as Fourier embeddings, no such reduction occurs. Within the Gaussian case,
715     the effective activation function depends on the history of the system’s global state through a two-time
716     correlation function if g > 0, but depends only on the instantaneous global state if g = 0, yielding a
717     more direct correspondence to an effective recurrent neural network. Note that g = 0 is necessary for the
718     weights to be minimum-norm with respect to the tuning curves they produce.
719         When specialized to continuous circular manifolds of fixed points, this framework provides a unifying
720     taxonomy of continuous-attractor models in which our model, classical models, the model of Darshan and
721     Rivkind [32], and the model of Beiran et al. [36] and Mastrogiuseppe and Ostojic [35] appear as special
722     cases. This taxonomy is based on three parameters: the rank K of the low-rank weights, the strength
723     g of random background weights, and the embedding type (Gaussian versus Fourier). For models with
724     Gaussian embeddings, such as ours, rotational symmetry of tuning curves produces rotational symmetry
725     of the effective recurrent interactions.
726         Within this taxonomy, we pinpoint the differences between our model and that of Darshan and
727     Rivkind [32]. Our model achieves tuning heterogeneity through multiple modes (K > 2), without
728     random background weights (g = 0), and a Gaussian embedding. The Darshan and Rivkind [32]
729     model achieves heterogeneity through two modes (K = 2), random background weights (g > 0), and
730     a structured embedding. These differences have important conceptual and functional consequences
731     described in Sec. S8 (see Sec. S8.1).

792     7     Discussion
793     Classical continuous-attractor models rely on continuously symmetric weights to generate homogeneous
794     tuning curves that tile a low-dimensional variable. Mammalian circuits exhibit far more heterogeneous

      bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.01.26.634933; this version posted October 26, 2025. The copyright holder for this

795     tuning, raising two questions: (1) what structure, if any, governs this heterogeneity, and (2) how do
796     networks generating such tuning relate to classical models? We addressed these questions by showing
797     that heterogeneous tuning curves in data are consistent with nonlinearly transformed samples from a
798     Gaussian process with rotationally symmetric correlation functions, and that these tuning curves can
799     comprise a stable attractor manifold by recurrent neural networks whose weight matrix is minimum-norm
800     subject to the tuning curve constraints. These networks admit a dynamical mean-field theory that recovers
801     the essential features of classical ring-attractor models, demonstrating that classical continuous-attractor
802     mechanisms could underlie heterogeneous tuning in mammalian circuits.

803     7.1 Is symmetry necessary?
804     The connection between our model and classical models relies on rotational symmetry of tuning curve
805     statistics. This raises the question: is symmetry necessary in continuous-attractor models? Strictly
806     speaking, no: the universal approximation property of recurrent neural networks [37] implies that
807     networks of N neurons can support a continuous attractor manifold of arbitrary shape embedded in a
808     K-dimensional subspace, provided that K is fixed and N can be taken arbitrarily large (Sec. A5).
809         What is the nature of the effective dynamics in such non-symmetric systems? Our dynamical
810     mean-field framework extends to asymmetric cases and reveals how systems maintain a continuum of
811     stable states without continuous symmetry. Fig. B7 examines systems with non-circulant correlation
812     functions Γx (θ, θ′ ) ̸= Γx (θ − θ′ ), parameterized by an asymmetry factor f ≥ 0. As f increases, the
813     effective weights J (θ, θ′ ) become increasingly non-circulant, yet the system maintains a one-dimensional
814     continuum of stable states. Stability is preserved through compensatory modulation in the gain kernel
815     U(θ, θ′ ) that appears in the quadratic term defining Q(t) in the effective activation function. The non-
816     circulant gain modulation exactly compensates for the non-circulant structure of the weights, enabling
817     continuous-attractor dynamics.
818         If symmetric manifolds are not strictly necessary, why might biological systems exhibit them? Head-
819     direction circuits appear relatively rigid, suggesting they may reflect lifetime-averaged environmental
820     statistics, which are angularly isotropic—there are no globally preferred directions. This isotropy is
821     broken within specific environments that animals explore over the short term, raising the question of
822     whether head-direction systems show asymmetries reflecting this on timescales of minutes to hours.
823     This would be an interesting experimental test. Additionally, while continuous-attractor models without
824     continuous symmetry can theoretically be constructed, they may be more difficult to form through
825     biologically plausible plasticity mechanisms. Existing models of ring-attractor self-organization [60]
826     produce symmetric weights, and extensions to asymmetric cases, though they may exist, have not been
827     presented to our knowledge.

828     7.2 From world structure to circuit structure
829     An intriguing question in neuroscience is: what can one infer about the structure of neural circuits from
830     the structure of the world? Near sensory periphery, such inferences are feasible. Recognizing that sounds
831     comprise superposed frequencies led Helmholtz to theorize that neural circuits decompose sounds into
832     frequencies, as occurs in the cochlea [61]. Perceptual observations led Hering to propose color opponency,
833     realized at the level of retinal ganglion cells and beyond [62]. In theoretical postdictions that could in
834     principle have been predictions, Olshausen and Field [63] showed that natural image statistics generate
835     V1-like receptive fields when represented by sparse, overcomplete neural populations. Several authors
836     have noted that the structure discovered by Buck and Axel [64] of many receptors, each binding to
837     multiple odorants resembles a compressed-sensing strategy for olfaction [65, 66].
838         Such inferences are more challenging deeper in the brain. However, consider the following argument
839     that generalizes the approach taken in this paper. Begin with a continuous variable in the world, such as
840     head direction, and assume that a fixed neural circuit maintains this variable by generating a persistent


                                                                       27
      bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.01.26.634933; this version posted October 26, 2025. The copyright holder for this
       preprint (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in
                                  perpetuity. It is made available under aCC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license.




841     activity manifold. This manifold must have the same topology as the variable (e.g., a circle for head
842     direction). What about its geometry? As argued above, environmental symmetries over an animal’s
843     lifetime may lead the distribution of neuronal tuning curves to obey the symmetry of the variable (e.g.,
844     rotational symmetry). This requirement is much weaker than supposing all tuning curves have identical
845     shapes up to shifts. At large N , this distributional symmetry becomes a geometric symmetry of the
846     manifold. The recurrent weights must encode the manifold’s structure to generate it through neuronal
847     dynamics. As we have shown, the geometric symmetry of the manifold appears in the weights as spectral
848     degeneracies (doubly degenerate eigenvalues for a circle, multiples of six for a torus with hexagonal
849     symmetry, and so on). Thus, on quite general grounds, we may expect symmetries of the world to be
850     reflected in the spectral structure of neural-circuit connectivity, even far from sensory periphery.
851         This argument proceeds from topology to geometry to connectivity: the topology of a variable
852     determines the topology of the neural manifold; environmental symmetries determine its geometry; and
853     this geometry produces degeneracies in the weight-matrix spectrum. We propose that such spectral
854     degeneracies are the best candidates for identifying continuous attractor structure in future mammalian
855     connectome data. Generalizing eigenvalue-based analysis techniques to more complex circuits than
856     considered here (e.g., with multiple cell types) is an important future direction.

857     7.3 Low-rank recurrent neural networks
858     The optimal weights (Eq. 3) exhibit an approximate low-rank structure, where the number of large
859     eigenvalues is much smaller than the number of neurons N . This situates our model within the framework
860     of low-rank recurrent neural networks, a well-studied class of models whose dynamics admit low-
861     dimensional mean-field descriptions [35, 36, 67, 68]. In Sec. A1, we show that distributional symmetries
862     in weight statistics generate corresponding symmetries in the effective low-dimensional dynamics (see also
863     [69]). Our model instantiates this principle: circular symmetry in the connectivity distribution produces
864     circular symmetry in the overlap dynamics. We further demonstrate that eigenvalue degeneracies, while
865     highly suggestive of underlying geometric structure, do not uniquely determine the symmetry group of
866     the dynamics without information about higher-order correlations in the weight statistics.
867         Previous work has proposed fitting low-rank network weights to neural data [67, 70], proceeding, via
868     empirical optimization, from tasks to weights that can then be analyzed theoretically. In contrast, our
869     approach provides an explicit analytical solution for the weights, enabling an end-to-end theory from
870     data statistics through weight structure to mean-field dynamics. We achieve this analytical tractability
871     by formulating continuous-attractor learning as a regression problem with a vanishing-flow constraint.
872     The more challenging problem of analytically describing learning for inherently dynamical tasks remains
873     largely open, though progress is being made [71].

874     7.4 Extension to general Lie groups
875     The heterogeneous-tuning network construction presented in this paper could be generalized to arbitrary
876     compact Lie groups beyond the circle and torus considered here. The remaining (compact, non-toroidal)
877     cases are non-abelian groups. As shown in Sec. A1, the resulting network models are expected to exhibit
878     eigenvalue degeneracies inherited from the dimensions of the irreducible representations that comprise
879     the group representation under which the connectivity transforms. These irreducible representations
880     generalize the Fourier modes used in the toroidal cases. Exploring continuous-attractor models with
881     heterogeneous tuning based on non-abelian symmetries represents an important avenue for future work.

882     7.5 From data to mean-field theories via generative processes
883     An interesting aspect of our analysis is the use of a statistical generative process to extend mean-
884     field approaches to data-derived network models. The overall pipeline we have established—from
885     characterizing single-neuron responses, to developing statistical models of neural heterogeneity, to


                                                                       28
      bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.01.26.634933; this version posted October 26, 2025. The copyright holder for this
       preprint (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in

886     deriving the necessary circuit mechanisms, to analyzing both the high-dimensional structure and mean-
887     field limit—provides a general framework for understanding how ordered computation emerges within
888     disordered neural circuits. It would be interesting to apply this pipeline to other brain regions and
889     computations. For example, Gaussian-process modeling of neural trajectories has been performed in the
890     motor system [72].
891         Several works prior to ours and Mainali et al. [29] have used nonlinearly transformed Gaussian
892     processes as generative processes for neuronal tuning within unsupervised “manifold learning” approaches
893     for neural data. Wu et al. [31] applied this approach to olfactory responses, learning a low-dimensional
894     Euclidean olfactory space and neuronal tuning curves over this space. Jensen et al. [30] developed a
895     more general framework for various manifolds, applying it to both fly and mouse head-direction neurons.
896     While these methods share our use of Gaussian processes for modeling neuronal tuning, they differ
897     fundamentally in approach. These methods simultaneously infer both latent-variable values and tuning
898     curves by computing posterior estimates within the generative process. In contrast, we sample tuning
899     curves from the prior distribution and assess whether their statistical features agree with tuning curves
900     computed from spiking data using existing knowledge of the encoded variable. Additionally, these
901     methods use maximum likelihood estimation to fit the full generative model, whereas we fit only the
902     correlation function to match the data.
903        These methods could be powerfully coupled with our framework. Such coupling would enable
904     application of our modeling and dynamical mean-field theory approach without prior knowledge of the
905     encoded variable, creating an unsupervised version of the present work. It would also provide principled
906     methods for fitting the parameters of the activation function and correlation function.

907     7.6 Order and disorder
908     The network models we have studied dynamically mimic classical continuous-attractor models while
909     representing a much broader class of implementations. The ordered, translation-invariant architectures
910     of classical models, corresponding to Fourier embeddings, represent only a special, measure-zero set
911     of solutions within this broader space. This may explain why heterogeneous tuning is prevalent in
912     larger brains such as those of mammals: it represents the generic case rather than a specialized solution.
913     This raises the question: What conditions might favor the emergence of classical, translation-invariant
914     architectures? One possibility is the constraint of limited neuronal resources. This could explain why
915     flies, with their smaller nervous systems, implement an anatomical ring-attractor model that closely
916     resembles classical models. The restricted number of available neurons may necessitate a more specialized,
917     symmetrical solution to achieve reliable continuous-attractor dynamics [6]. As for structural perturbations,
918     analysis of structural robustness shows that our disordered networks maintain, or fail to maintain, their
919     attractor properties under weight perturbations comparably to classical circulant models (Sec. B5.1).

1205     A1.4 Do eigenvalue degeneracies imply dynamical symmetries?
1206     Eigenvalue degeneracies, while highly suggestive of underlying symmetry, do not strictly determine
1207     the symmetry group of the dynamics. This is because eigenvalue degeneracies depend only on the
1208     second-order statistics of the loading vectors, whereas the symmetry group of the dynamics is determined
1209     by the full distribution P (u, v).

1211     Gaussian with the same second-order moments as in our actual model. This Gaussianization preserves

1218          There is an interesting caveat, however: in the Gaussian construction described here, while the
1219     dynamics possess the product-group symmetry (SO(2))D , only a single two-dimensional subspace
1220     corresponding to the highest-variance doublet is stable, and the projections onto the remaining two-
1221     dimensional subspaces decay to zero. To demonstrate this, we constructed a rank-four network (K = 4)
1222     where, for simplicity, corresponding columns of U and V are perfectly correlated (i.e., U = V ), with
1223     the first two columns having variance 10 and the second two having variance 5. The overlap vector κ(t)
1224     is four-dimensional. We initialized the system along a ring in either the dominant subspace (first doublet)
1225     or the subdominant subspace (second doublet). In both cases, only the components of κ(t) corresponding
1226     to the dominant subspace survive asymptotically, and we observe quasi-continuous-attractor behavior
1227     (Fig. A1). This winner-take-all behavior is analogous to that seen in networks with Gaussian loadings
1228     storing discrete fixed points [36]; our construction represents a generalization of this result to continuous
1229     manifolds.
1230         To conclude: While eigenvalue degeneracies are highly non-generic features and thus serve as
1231     suggestive indicators of potential dynamical symmetry, they do not uniquely specify the symmetry group
1232     of the dynamics without additional information about higher-order structure. Although the eigenvectors
1233     V x and V ϕ appear disordered in scatterplots (Fig. 7F, Fig. B6), and thus naive techniques such as spectral
1234     embedding do not work, they contain higher-order correlations that encode the SO(2) symmetry. Whether
1235     this structure can be reliably extracted from data remains an open question. Eigenvalue degeneracies
1236     appear to be the most robust and readily identifiable signature of continuous-attractor organization in
1237     connectivity data.

1285     A3       Details of large-N analysis of optimized weights
1286     A3.1 Fourier conventions in Sec. 4
1287     The eigenvalues of a circulant kernel matrix—for example, Γx (θ−θ′ ), where θ and θ′ serve as the indices—
1288     are given by the Fourier transform of Γx (∆θ) in the complex exponential basis. These eigenvalues have
1289     doublet degeneracy, Γ̂xn = Γ̂x−n , where n = −D, . . . , D. Within each two-dimensional degenerate
1290     subspace, we are free to choose any eigenbasis. In Sec. 4 of the main text (whose details are given
1291     below) and Sec. A1 of the Theory Appendix, we adopt a sine-cosine basis, which is a rotation of the
1292     complex-exponential basis, because we require real singular vectors for the analysis.
1293        Note that, when using this basis, we do not use the sine-cosine Fourier coefficients of Γx (∆θ). These
1294     coefficients possess only nonzero cosine terms and thus are not the correct eigenvalues of the kernel
1295     matrix, which come from the complex-exponential Fourier transform.

1439     A5       Existence of continuous attractors with arbitrary manifolds via uni-
1440              versal approximation
1441     Consider dynamics of a K-dimensional vector κ(t) governed by

                                                       τ ∂t κ(t) = −κ(t) + f (κ(t)),                                                 (99)

1442     where f : RK → RK is a continuous evolution function. The decay term −κ(t) imposes no loss of
1443     generality. These dynamics may possess a continuous attractor manifold of dimension ≤ K.
1444        Universal approximation (see, e.g., Chong [73]) guarantees that, for sufficiently large N , one can find
1445     N × K matrices U and V such that
                                                  f (κ) ≈ V T ϕ(U κ)                                          (100)
1446     to arbitrary accuracy uniformly over κ restricted to a compact set (e.g., encompassing the explored state
1447     space of the dynamics), provided that ϕ(·) is non-polynomial. Defining x(t) = U κ(t) yields

                                                    τ ∂t x(t) = −x(t) + U V T ϕ(x(t)),                                              (101)

1448     which is a rank-K recurrent neural network embedding the original dynamical system in a K-dimensional
1449     subspace spanned by the columns of U .
1450        One might worry that choosing U to implement the approximation distorts the geometry of the
1451     κ(t) dynamics through a non-orthonormal embedding. However, the approximation property holds with
1452     probability one even when elements of U are chosen randomly [73], in which case the columns of U
1453     become orthogonal at large N .
1454         The construction used here of a low-rank recurrent neural network that emulates a given dynamical
1455     system by representing the evolution function via a one-hidden layer neural network is essentially the
1456     same as that proposed by the Neural Engineering Framework [74]. The random-U construction suggested
1457     above would result in a ring-attractor model with a random embedding.

1658     B3.2 Non-saturating and saturating activation functions
1659     For the data-derived network model (Setting 1) and generative process (Setting 2), we use unbounded
1660     activation functions for the following reasons.

1661         1. This choice is more neurobiologically realistic: cortical neurons typically operate below saturation
1662            in their dynamic range [75].
1663         2. The experimental tuning curves show no obvious signs of saturation at high activation levels,
1664            making estimation of a saturation point ill-posed.

1665         For theoretical analysis (Setting 3), we switch to bounded nonlinearities for the following reasons
1666     discussed in the introduction to Sec. 4.

1667         1. This enables closed-form evaluation of certain integrals in the mean-field derivations, e.g., the
1668            function G(C11 , C22 , C12 ) (Sec. A4.1, Eq. 66).
1669         2. Dynamically, saturation precludes runaway activity, removing the need for the uniform inhibition
1670            parameter c used in Sec. 2.3.
1671         3. Since we drop the soft threshold b, firing rates are non-sparse over θ and the normalization
1672            functional Z[x⋆ ] is not needed to amplify small tuning features.

1673         Within each class (non-saturating or saturating), the specific functional form is not critical: different
1674     non-saturating nonlinearities (e.g., ReLU versus softplus) or different saturating nonlinearities (e.g., tanh
1675     versus error function) would not meaningfully alter network behavior or theoretical conclusions.

1778     Supplement: Dynamics of learned attractors
1779     S1      Introduction
1780     Here, we revisit reservoir-computing recurrent neural networks that are first trained with open-loop,
1781     teacher-forced inputs before operating autonomously in closed-loop mode. We consider the closed-
1782     loop dynamics resulting from open-loop learning of fixed points. Rivkind and Barak [33] analyzed
1783     local stability around fixed points by computing a response function whose poles correspond to outlier
1784     eigenvalues of the Jacobian matrix. Their approach has three limitations. First, it addresses only local
1785     linear stability, providing no description of global nonlinear dynamics. Second, the relationship between
1786     response-function poles and the full nonlinear dynamics remains unclear. Third, their analysis relies on
1787     heuristic Gaussianity assumptions that prevent generalization to structured reservoir architectures.
1788          We revisit this problem through dynamical mean-field theory. Using a path-integral formulation, we
1789     derive equations describing the global nonlinear dynamics through order parameters: overlap functions
1790     mµ (t) that depend on a single time variable and correlation functions C(t, t′ ) that depend on two times.
1791     For Gaussian feedback weights, the dynamical mean-field description reduces to an effective recurrent
1792     neural network of P units, where P is the number of prescribed fixed points. Each unit represents an
1793     order parameter mµ (t) measuring the overlap between the high-dimensional network state and a specific
1794     learned pattern. These units interact through an effective weight matrix determined by target-signal
1795     statistics and use an activation function that, for g > 0, depends on the full history of the system through
1796     the two-time correlation function C(t, t′ ).
1797         By linearizing these equations around fixed points, we demonstrate that the order-parameter dynamics
1798     are governed by a Jacobian whose eigenvalues precisely match the poles of the response function
1799     computed by Rivkind and Barak [33]. This approach eliminates heuristic assumptions, extends naturally
1800     to structured reservoirs, provides a complete description of the nonlinear dynamics, and establishes a
1801     clear interpretation of response-function poles as eigenvalues governing order-parameter dynamics.
1802          While we focus on the general problem, this formulation can be specialized to continuous manifolds
1803     of fixed points, as done by Darshan and Rivkind [32] for ring attractors.
1804         In general, this supplement is self-contained and independent of the main text, though we sometimes
1805     refer to it, particularly in Sec. S8 where we provide a taxonomy of ring-attractor models. Variables and
1806     parameters are normally represented by the same symbols as those in the main text to which they are
1807     analogous, with the exception of what we call here Γµν and Ψµν . The former is analogous to Γϕ (θ, θ′ ) in
1808     the main text, and the latter is analogous to Γx (θ, θ′ ). In contrast to the main text, we set the single-neuron
1809     time constant τ = 1 (equivalently, measuring time in units of τ ).

1961         Case 5 requires a couple of comments. First, Beiran et al. [36] and Mastrogiuseppe and Ostojic
1962     [35] constructed a rank-two ring-attractor model using jointly Gaussian loadings with appropriately
1963     configured correlations. In case 5, U is marginally Gaussian but the full joint distribution over U and
1964     V is non-Gaussian due to the nonlinearity (Sec. A1). As explained in Sec. A1, this difference is minor
1965     for K = 2, as in case 5 (since, for both Gaussian and non-Gaussian loadings, the symmetry group of
1966     the loading distribution is the circle) but becomes pertinent for K > 2 (in which case Gaussian and
1967     non-Gaussian loadings lead to different symmetry groups for the loading distribution).
1968         Second, Mastrogiuseppe and Ostojic [35] studied the same ring-attractor structure as in Beiran et al.
1969     [36] with added i.i.d. random connectivity scaled by g > 0. While this might appear equivalent to
1970     case 7 in our taxonomy (which reduces to case 5 as g → 0), they differ in the following way. In
1971     Mastrogiuseppe and Ostojic [35], the low-rank and i.i.d. components are independent, analogous to the
1972     circulant-plus-noise constructions examined in Sec. 2.3 and Sec. 4.2, though using a Gaussian rather than
1973     Fourier embedding. In contrast, the framework upon which the taxonomy is based involves learning in an
1974     open-loop setting where the low-rank component is correlated with Jij .

1975     S8.1 Comparison to Darshan and Rivkind model
1976     Recall that our model achieves tuning heterogeneity through multiple Fourier modes (K > 2), without
1977     random background weights (g = 0), and a Gaussian embedding; while the Darshan and Rivkind [32]
1978     model achieves heterogeneity through two modes (K = 2), random background weights (g > 0), and
1979     a Fourier embedding. The conceptual and functional consequences that result from these differences
1980     include the following.

1981          • Interpretability of effective dynamics. Because the Darshan and Rivkind [32] model uses a
1982            Fourier embedding, its dynamical mean-field theory does not reduce to an effective recurrent neural
1983            network, obscuring its relationship to classical continuous-attractor dynamics. Furthermore, even
1984            with a Gaussian embedding, g > 0 would prevent such interpretation due to history dependence in
1985            the effective activation function. While this limitation limits theoretical understanding, it is not a
1986            functional problem.

1987          • Stability under increasing heterogeneity. The Darshan and Rivkind [32] model is non-minimum-
1988            norm and operates near the chaotic regime as its mechanism for generating tuning heterogeneity.
1989            That introduces the functional problem that, once tuning curves deviate substantially from cosine-
1990            like profiles, chaotic fluctuations destabilize the manifold, preventing its use as a memory system
1991            (Fig. B12)—though unlike circulant-plus-noise constructions (Sec. 2.3), collapse to a few discrete
1992            fixed points is avoided in the non-chaotic regime. Given tuning curves produced by the Darshan
1993            and Rivkind [32] model, one could apply the minimum-norm prescription to obtain a version


                                                                        74
       bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.01.26.634933; this version posted October 26, 2025. The copyright holder for this
        preprint (which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in
                                   perpetuity. It is made available under aCC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license.




1994             of our model with those tuning-curve statistics, thereby eliminating chaotic instability at large
1995             heterogeneity. This raises the question of why a neural circuit would implement the Darshan
1996             and Rivkind [32] weight matrix. One possibility is that operating near chaotic instability proves
1997             computationally useful in circuits that must combine memory of a continuous variable with dynamic
1998             computations.
1999          • Flexibility for matching experimental data. The Darshan and Rivkind [32] model produces
2000            only a one-dimensional family of tuning-curve statistics parameterized by g. In contrast, our
2001            minimum-norm prescription allows specification of arbitrary correlation functions for input currents,
2002            providing greater flexibility for matching experimental observations. While not infinitely flexible,
2003            this approach provides sufficient degrees of freedom to achieve quantitatively accurate fits to
2004            experimental head-direction tuning curves with only three fitted parameters.
